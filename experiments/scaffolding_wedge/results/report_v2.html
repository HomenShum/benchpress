First Principle

From the Dive-into-Claude-Code architecture analysis:

"As frontier models converge in capability, the operational harness becomes the differentiator. Less capable models need more scaffolding than more capable models."

The Anthropic advisor pattern codifies this: Opus provides scaffolding (plans, corrections, stop signals) that Sonnet/Haiku lack.

The question this experiment asks

Can we automate that scaffolding delivery? If we distill Pro's reasoning pattern into a structured skill template, and inject it into Flash's context, does the cheaper model behave like the more capable one?

V2 fix: stripped the context brief. Models get only the query. The ONLY scaffolding that varies between configurations is the distilled skill. Now we're testing reasoning transfer, not context richness.

CRITICAL ALERT: The FDA Food Code mandates that refrigerated perishable foods must be kept at or below 41°F. At 52°F, your product is in the "Temperature Danger Zone" where harmful bacteria multiply rapidly. Treat this as a severe food safety and high-shrink emergency.

1. Best Guess at the Underlying Issue

A temperature of 52°F indicates a total loss of cooling capacity rather than a minor fluctuation. The most likely culprits are:
• Mechanical Failure: A blown compressor, a severe refrigerant leak, or a broken evaporator fan.
• Frozen Evaporator Coils: Often caused by a failed defrost timer or blocked airflow, causing the system to freeze over and stop blowing cold air.
• Operational Error: The cooler door was propped open during a large delivery and left unattended, or a tripped electrical breaker has cut power to the unit.

2. Specific Actions (Immediate & Follow-Up)

Immediate Actions (Next 15-30 Minutes):

• Quarantine the Cooler: Shut the door, remove any door props, and tape a "DO NOT OPEN - OUT OF TEMP" sign on the door. Every time the door opens, you lose residual cold air.
• Check the Basics: Verify the door seal is intact, check the electrical breaker box to ensure the cooler hasn't tripped, and look to see if the fans are spinning or if there is a massive block of ice on the ceiling unit.
• Dispatch Maintenance: Submit an emergency "Priority 1" work order to your Facilities/Maintenance team for immediate refrigeration repair. (Refer to your *Emergency Maintenance Dispatch Policy*).
• Assess Product Viability: If you know *for a fact* the temperature rose above 41°F less than 2 hours ago, immediately relocate high-risk items (raw meat, seafood, dairy, deli) to other working walk-ins, reach-in coolers, or refrigerated trailers. *If you do not know when it went out of temp, leave the product where it is until QA can advise.*

Follow-Up Actions:

• Log Temperatures: Use a calibrated probe thermometer to take the *internal* temperature of the food (e.g., stick it inside a carton of milk or between two packages of ground beef), not just the ambient air. Document these temps.
• Execute Shrink/Spoilage Protocols: Once the unit is fixed, you will likely need to discard product. Follow your *Food Safety Temperature Abuse Policy* and *Inventory Shrink/Salvage Policy* to scan out destroyed items so your inventory system remains accurate.

3. Information to Gather for Investigation

Before you get on the phone with leadership or Food Safety, gather the following data:
• The Timeline: Check your manual temperature logs or automated sensor dashboard. What time was the cooler last recorded at 41°F or below? (This determines the "Time Out of Temperature" window, which dictates what food can be saved).
• Internal Product Temps: What is the actual internal temperature of the most vulnerable products in the cooler right now?
• Visual/Auditory Symptoms: Is the unit completely silent? Is it blowing warm air? Is there a puddle of water on the floor?
• Financial Exposure: Roughly estimate the value and categories of the product inside (e.g., "It's our main meat cooler, holding roughly $15,000 in fresh poultry and beef").

4. Escalation & Cross-Functional Coordination Needed

• Facilities/Refrigeration Vendor: Needs to be on-site immediately to diagnose and repair the mechanical failure.
• Food Safety / Quality Assurance (QA): Escalate to your regional Food Safety manager. They must make the final, legally compliant call on what product can be salvaged and what must be destroyed. Do not throw away or keep product without their sign-off.

• Asset Protection / Risk Management: If the product loss is severe (usually over a specific dollar threshold, like $5,000+), you will need to file a claim under your *Spoilage Insurance/Asset Loss Policy*. They will need your temperature logs and the repair invoice to process the claim.
